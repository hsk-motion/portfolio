02Core Research — M.S. Thesis

How shoe cushioning rewrites joint stiffness strategy.

Humans change their posture in response to changing external environments to adjust the effects on the body.

— Arampatzis, 2002; Devita & Skelly, 1992
Δ Force Δ COM
= Vertical Stiffness (McMahon & Cheng, 1990)
Midsole cushioning was isolated as the test variable — material, height, hardness, and shape — using a controlled shoe platform (Asker C-70 to C-40 hardness range).
Ankle Stiffness
↑ Increased
As midsole hardness decreased, ankle stiffness rose to compensate.
Knee Stiffness
↓ Decreased
The knee offloaded stiffness as the ankle took on a stabilizing role.
Vertical / COM Stiffness
— No Difference
Whole-body stiffness stayed constant — the adjustment happened locally, joint by joint.

Softer midsoles increased ankle stiffness and contact displacement while leaving overall vertical stiffness unchanged — suggesting the body redistributes joint-level strategy to maintain general stability during recreational running. (Kulmala et al., 2018; Liu, Wu & Lam, 2017)

03Publications & Presentations

Peer-reviewed work on footwear, stiffness, and gait.

Journal Article — Applied Sciences, 2025
The Effect of Different Midsole Cushioning Types on Impact Forces and Joint Stiffness in Heel-toe Runners
Hyeong-Sik Kim, Sang-Kyoon Park · Full-funded by FILA
Result
Softer cushioning was associated with increased ankle stiffness, contributing to more stable gait.
Insight
Stiffness reflects muscle and soft-tissue motion, limiting its representation of true mechanical stiffness — a neuromechanical investigation is required.
